History of St. Kizito

Patron Saint of Children & Youth

The Youngest Witness

1886

Feast Day: June 3rd

"I will give you my life for the sake of the King. I am not afraid to die for Him who died for me."

Early Life

Kizito was born in the Kingdom of Buganda, in present-day Uganda, during the late 19th century. As a young page in the court of King Mwanga II, he was known for his joyful spirit and dedication to his duties. His name, originally Omuto, reflected his youth and vitality within the royal enclosure.

Though the court was a place of high status, it was also a place of great spiritual turmoil. Kizito was introduced to Christianity by the Catholic missionaries, the White Fathers, and later by St. Charles Lwanga, who became a mentor and spiritual father to the young pages.

Martyrdom

The climate in the kingdom shifted dramatically when King Mwanga began to perceive the Christian faith as a threat to his absolute authority. In May 1886, the King demanded that all followers of Christ renounce their faith or face death.

Kizito, at only thirteen or fourteen years old, was among those who refused to yield. On the morning of June 3, 1886, at Namugongo, he was led to the pyre. Eyewitnesses recorded that the boy went to his death with a smile, praying and encouraging his fellow martyrs. He was the youngest of the twenty-two Catholic Uganda Martyrs.

Legacy

Pope Benedict XV beatified the martyrs in 1920, and they were canonized by Pope Paul VI in 1964. Today, St. Kizito is venerated as a beacon of courage for youth worldwide. His life serves as a reminder that holiness is not reserved for the elderly or the influential, but is accessible to the pure of heart.

Our parish, named in his honor, strives to emulate his simple faith and unwavering commitment to the Gospel. Through our community life, we keep the light of his witness burning for future generations.
